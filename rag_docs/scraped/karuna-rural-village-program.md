# Karuna Rural Village Program - Dharamsala Animal Rescue
Source: https://dharamsalaanimalrescue.org/karuna-rural-village-program

## My Village is safe and compassionate. No rabies. No dog bites. No cruelty

### “Karuna” means Compassion

The goal of this program is to create safe and compassionate villages.

Many rural villages in India have beliefs that are outdated and are potentially causing more harm than good when it comes to their interactions with stray/community dogs. Our goal is to create a cycle of benefit for both the villages/villagers and the dogs that reside there.

Our first step is to hold meetings and sessions with women’s groups and children within each village. These sessions will not only be a way to educate on rabies/bite safety and compassion, but be a discovery session for us to learn what issues the people are facing and how we can solve them peacefully.

Next, we want to enlist influencers in the community that can help us create harmony. Currently, humans get bit due to being taught to throw rocks at dogs, and dogs get poisoned by farmers who don’t want them attacking their animals.

To ease and manage conflict, we will show how feeding the dogs as opposed to being cruel will decrease dog bites and fear. Both people and dogs will feel safer and happier, hopefully a bond will develop.

As the the relationship develops, we will work to sterilize and vaccinate the dogs in the village who are now fed and cared for.

The final step, we hope, will be a rabies and cruelty free environment, with families adopting stray dogs and who know how to care for them.

The cycle of benefit will continue.

### How Gender Inequality Impacts Health

#### What happens when a girl gets bit by an unknown dog?

One important indicator of gender inequality in India is the lack of autonomy among women to go outside the home. Permission must be granted to visit a friend, go to a store, etc (most likey by a male member of the family). So what happens if a young girl sneaks out to play with her friends and gets bit?

In our first sessions, we learned a terrifying fact. Most girls do not tell a parent if they were bit by a dog because they feared the punishment they would get for leaving the house when they were not supposed to. This inequality, could lead to death by rabies if the dog was infected.

To encourage safety in communication, we attended a Maa/beti (mother/daughter) Mela program held by Jagori Rural Charitable Trust . DAR had a booth where the women and children could come learn about what we do, what to do if they were bit, how to interact with dogs in a kind and compassionate manner, and we emphasized the importance of having a good mother/daughter relationship so daughters feel ok to tell her parents she was bitten ,even if it meant she left the house when she was not supposed to.
