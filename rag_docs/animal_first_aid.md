## Why First Aid Matters

Animal hospitals and rescue centres are sometimes too far away to reach quickly during an emergency. Learning basic animal first aid allows you to stabilise a dog's condition while waiting for professional help or transport.

**Important:** First aid is not a substitute for veterinary care. Always seek professional attention as soon as possible.

## Assessing a Stray Dog in Distress

Before approaching, assess the situation from a safe distance:
- Is the dog conscious and responsive?
- Is there visible bleeding?
- Is the dog able to stand or move?
- Is the dog showing signs of aggression (even injured dogs may bite out of fear)?

**Always prioritise your own safety.** An injured or frightened dog may bite even if usually friendly.

## Basic First Aid Steps

### Bleeding Wounds
1. Apply firm, direct pressure with a clean cloth or bandage.
2. Do not remove the cloth if it soaks through — add more material on top.
3. Keep pressure applied for at least 5–10 minutes.
4. Do not use a tourniquet unless trained to do so.

### Suspected Fractures
- Do not attempt to set a broken bone.
- Restrict the dog's movement to prevent further injury.
- If the dog must be moved, support the entire body — use a flat board or rigid surface as a stretcher if available.
- Keep the dog warm and calm while waiting for help.

### Unconscious Dog
- Check for breathing — look for chest rise, feel for breath at the nose.
- If breathing is absent and you are trained in animal CPR, you may attempt it.
- Place the dog on its side, keep the airway clear.
- Seek emergency veterinary care immediately.

### Heatstroke (common in summer months)
- Move the dog to shade immediately.
- Apply cool (not cold) water to the body, especially the neck, armpits, and groin.
- Offer small amounts of water if the dog is conscious.
- Fan the dog while applying water to speed evaporative cooling.
- Get to a vet — heatstroke can be fatal even after the dog appears to recover.

### Poisoning
- Do not induce vomiting unless instructed by a vet.
- Try to identify what the dog ate (take a photo if possible).
- Contact a veterinarian immediately.

## 7 Warning Signs That Require Emergency Vet Attention

1. **Difficulty breathing** — laboured breathing, blue or pale gums, open-mouth breathing in a dog.
2. **Severe or uncontrolled bleeding** — bleeding that does not slow after 10 minutes of pressure.
3. **Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness**.
4. **Suspected broken bones** — especially if the dog cannot bear weight or a limb is at an abnormal angle.
5. **Seizures** — convulsions, uncontrolled shaking, loss of bladder/bowel control.
6. **Signs of severe pain** — yelping constantly, inability to be still, guarding a body part intensely.
7. **Suspected poisoning** — vomiting repeatedly, drooling excessively, collapse, sudden disorientation.

## Moving an Injured Dog Safely

- Approach slowly and speak in a calm, low voice.
- Use a muzzle if you have one — even a friendly dog may bite when in pain.
- A strip of cloth can be used as a makeshift muzzle (loop around the snout, tie behind the ears) — do not use on a dog that is vomiting or having difficulty breathing.
- Support the chest and hindquarters when lifting.
- Transport in a box or on a flat surface if possible.

## When to Call Dharamsala Animal Rescue

Contact DAR for any street dog that is:
- Visibly injured and unable to move.
- Unconscious or semi-conscious.
- Showing signs of severe illness.
- A nursing mother with puppies at risk.
- Involved in a road accident.
