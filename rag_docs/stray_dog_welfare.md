## Why Stray Animals Exist

Stray dog populations are high throughout India and other developing countries for a combination of reasons:

- **Abandoned pets** — dogs surrendered by owners due to moving, cost, illness, or changing circumstances.
- **Born on the street** — offspring of unsterilised strays reproducing without intervention.
- **Escaped or lost animals** that were never recovered.
- **Lack of widespread spay/neuter access** — without affordable and accessible sterilisation programmes, populations grow rapidly. A single unsterilised pair of dogs can produce up to 67,000 offspring in 6 years.

## The Dog-Human Conflict

India has a long and complex relationship with street dogs. Street dogs occupy an important ecological niche (scavenging, territorial behaviour that limits incursion by wildlife) but also create public health risks (rabies, bites) and welfare concerns (injury, starvation).

The conflict intensifies when:
- Rabies outbreaks occur in an area.
- A dog or pack attacks a person or child.
- Resources (food, space) become scarce.

Animal shelters and rescue organisations like DAR operate at the interface of this conflict — providing triage for injured dogs, vaccination to control rabies, sterilisation to manage population growth, and community education to reduce fear and promote coexistence.

## ABCAR — Animal Birth Control and Anti-Rabies Programme

Dharamsala Animal Rescue runs the **ABCAR programme** (Animal Birth Control and Anti-Rabies), which is the humane and scientifically proven approach to managing street dog populations:

1. **Catch** — humanely trap street dogs.
2. **Vaccinate** — administer anti-rabies vaccination.
3. **Sterilise** — surgically sterilise (spay/neuter) to prevent reproduction.
4. **Return** — release the dog back to its territory.

Sterilised and vaccinated dogs are identified by an ear notch. These dogs maintain their territories, preventing unvaccinated, unsterilised dogs from moving in — making the programme self-reinforcing over time.

## Why Culling Does Not Work

Mass killing of street dogs is not an effective solution and is actually counterproductive:

- New, unvaccinated dogs quickly fill the territory vacated by removed animals.
- It does not address the root causes (food availability, human waste, abandonment).
- It is opposed by the WHO and WOAH as ineffective for rabies control.
- It is inhumane and illegal under Indian law (Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act).

The only proven, sustainable method for reducing stray dog populations and rabies is ABCAR combined with community education.

## How the Community Can Help

- Support vaccination and sterilisation camps when they come to your area.
- Do not abandon pets — contact DAR or a local shelter if you can no longer care for your animal.
- Secure food waste — accessible garbage is the primary reason dogs congregate in human areas.
- Report injured, sick, or aggressive dogs to DAR rather than taking independent action.
- Educate children about safe behaviour around street dogs.

## Dharamsala Animal Rescue's Work

DAR operates in the Dharamsala region (including McLeod Ganj, Dharamkot, and the surrounding Kangra Valley area) providing:
- Emergency rescue for injured street dogs.
- Veterinary care and rehabilitation.
- ABCAR sterilisation and vaccination programme.
- Community and school education on dog safety and rabies.
- Adoption facilitation for dogs that cannot survive independently.
