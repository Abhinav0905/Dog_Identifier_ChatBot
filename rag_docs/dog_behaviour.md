## Reading Dog Body Language

Understanding what a dog is communicating through its body can help you avoid dangerous situations and interact safely with street dogs.

### Relaxed / Friendly Dog
- Loose, wiggly body posture.
- Tail wagging broadly (tail height depends on breed, but broad movement indicates friendliness).
- Soft eyes, relaxed mouth.
- May approach you directly, sniff your hand.
- Play bow (front legs down, rear up) is a clear invitation to interact.

### Fearful Dog
- Body low to the ground, trying to make itself smaller.
- Tail tucked between legs.
- Ears flat against the head.
- Avoiding eye contact, turning head away.
- May move away from you.

**Important:** A fearful dog is unpredictable. If cornered, a fearful dog will bite in self-defence. Never corner or block the retreat path of a frightened dog. Give it space and a clear escape route.

### Stressed or Anxious Dog
- Yawning, lip-licking repeatedly.
- Panting when it is not hot.
- Excessive sniffing of the ground when you are nearby.
- Whale eye (showing the whites of the eyes).

### Aggressive / Warning Signs
- Stiff body, weight shifted forward.
- Tail raised and stiff (not wagging loosely).
- Hackles raised along the spine.
- Direct, hard eye contact (staring).
- Low growl or bark.
- Wrinkled muzzle, bared teeth.
- Lunging forward.

If you observe these signs, **do not run**. Running triggers prey drive and can cause the dog to chase. Back away slowly and calmly, avoiding direct eye contact.

## How to Approach a Stray Dog Safely

1. Move slowly and calmly — stop and let the dog notice you before you approach.
2. Crouch down to appear smaller and less threatening.
3. Turn your body slightly sideways rather than facing the dog head-on.
4. Avoid direct eye contact — let your gaze be soft rather than fixed.
5. Extend the back of your hand low for the dog to sniff if it chooses to approach.
6. Allow the dog to come to you — do not reach out over its head.
7. If the dog retreats or shows discomfort, do not follow.

## Getting a Scared Dog to Trust You

Building trust with a traumatised or fearful street dog takes time and patience.

- Consistency is key — visit the same location at the same time if possible.
- Offer food at a distance first; gradually move the offering closer over multiple visits.
- Never force interaction.
- Avoid sudden movements, loud voices, or direct approaches.
- Sit or crouch nearby and let the dog observe you at a distance.
- Progress at the dog's pace — a dog that chooses to approach you has already made a significant breakthrough.

## When a Dog Jumps on You

Street dogs will sometimes jump up on people, particularly if they associate humans with food.

- Turn away and cross your arms — do not push the dog, as pushing can be interpreted as play.
- Only give attention (eye contact, speech, petting) when the dog has all four paws on the ground.
- Be consistent — the same response every time is essential for the dog to learn.

## Dogs and Children

Children are disproportionately affected by dog bites because they:
- Make sudden, unpredictable movements.
- Make high-pitched sounds that can trigger a chase response.
- Often approach dogs head-on and make direct eye contact.
- May not recognise warning signs in dog body language.

Teach children to:
- Always ask an adult before approaching any dog.
- Never approach a dog that is eating, sleeping, or with puppies.
- Stay still like a tree if a dog approaches and they are unsure.
- Never run from a dog.
